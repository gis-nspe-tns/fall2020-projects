Deforestation Patterns in Indigenous and Non-Indigenous Territories in the Brazilian Amazon

Increasingly, research findings suggest humans occupied the Amazon Basin long before colonial assertions of “Terra Nullius”. In Brazil, an indigenous territory is an area inhabited and exclusively managed by the indigenous peoples. Article 231 of the Brazilian Constitution recognizes the inalienable right of indigenous peoples to lands they "traditionally occupy" and automatically confers them permanent possession of these lands. However, these territories continue to be dispossessed for infrastructure, mining, and agribusiness that are leading the Amazonian destruction. Due to these extractive and exhaustive activities driving climate crises, Amazon rainforest may be turning into risk geographies faster than previously thought. This spatial analysis explores the deforestation patterns in Indigenous and Non-Indigenous Territories in the Brazillian Amazon and concludes that Indigenous Territories, managed by the indigenous Amazonian peoples, serve as a strong barrier to deforestation.
