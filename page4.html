Green Space & Violent Crime - New York City

Violent crime in New York City has long been a prevalent issue. The recent Covid-19 pandemic has exacerbated trends in violent crime throughout the city. Recent analysis, however, suggests how the presence of green spaces in communities can actually help to reduce instances of violent crime. While crime reduction tactics often focus on punitive, criminalization measures, recent research suggests the possibility of considering crime in terms of spatial analysis. This project seeks to uncover the correlation between rates of green space in New York City community districts, and instances of violent crime. Utilizing New York City Open Data, this analysis identifies an inverse relationship between green space and violent crime - communities with higher levels of green space are more likely to have lower rates of violent crime. This project additionally considers other demographic factors, such as poverty and rent burden, which are likely contributors to crime in communities. The findings of this project are relevant for policymakers and city planners to consider the role of green spaces in ensuring public safety.

Mapping Gentrification in Mexico City

As in many cities around the world, gentrification is a key issue in Mexico City. In recent years, the city has fallen prey to a construction boom and the cost of living has greatly increased. This building boom has gone hand in hand with the rebranding of the city as a trendy tourist destination and the rise of Airbnb. This project aims to map risks of gentrification in Mexico City neighborhoods and identify what areas are most at risk within the city. We will center on the following question: Can a series of spatial variables help us determine gentrification risk? What factors make a neighborhood desirable for gentrifiers? What factors put residents at risk of being priced out?

Counties of Hunger: Food Deserts in Florida

This project features mapping and analysis of the proximity of grocery stores to vulnerable Floridians who live below the federal poverty level. Mapping highlights demographic pockets of Florida that have an increased need for access to fresh, nutritious food. The project seeks to expand on the existing research that correlates a person’s poverty level to their narrow access to healthy food options. Areas with the strongest need include communities in Baker County, Columbia County, Gilchrist County, Bradford County, Franklin County, and Union County. Increased development of grocery stores in these counties would be an invaluable addition to the livelihoods of the people living there.

The Politics of Pollution: Mapping Toxic Pollutant Risk in New Jersey

In the face of a global public health crisis, there is no better time to consider the ways in which one’s lived environment protects or hinders one’s chances at a full and healthy life. This spatial analysis examines this issue through the lens of man-made pollution in the state of New Jersey and its unequal risk of producing negative health consequences across vulnerable populations. Synthesis of spatial data will thoroughly examine the relationship between hazard and social vulnerability via risk scoring. Within this analysis, I identify 7 counties with very highly elevated risk of negative health effects due to toxic pollutant exposure based on data from the CDC Social Vulnerability Index (SVI) and New Jersey Department of Environmental Protection (DEP). This information is meant to provide researchers, policymakers, and community members alike with the information necessary to make positive change toward reducing the effects of environmental inequality.

Deforestation in Indigenous Territories on Turtle Island

Indigenous Peoples and the communities that remain on federally-recognized Native lands in the United States are continuously left out of conversations on climate change and land degradation, specifically how it relates to their sovereignty and cultural survival (Whyte, 2017). Although climate change affects all citizens on Turtle Island (United States), it is reported that native communities are disproportionately affected by climate change due to the location of federally-allocated lands and their reliance on natural resources (National Congress of American Indians, 2020). One of the major risks for Indigenous Peoples and their territories is deforestation and the disruption of their food systems, as many communities rely on hunting, fishing, and foraging—all practices that depend on healthy forest growth. This GIS spatial analysis includes a loss summary of forest cover from the years of 2001-2019 within federally-recognized Native American territories. This research is critical for Indigenous land management of their sovereign nations and ecosystems. Within this analysis, I identify the territories with the most forest cover in 2001 and the most forest loss from 2001-2019, using data provided by the National Land Cover Database and United States Department of Agriculture. This map provides Indigenous communities and researchers with spatial analysis recognizing the Native territories in the US that are most at risk of deforestation.
